What are Invasive Species?

What do Pablo Escobar’s escaped hippos in Colombia and cats in Antarctica have in common? They are both declared invasive in their respective areas.
Invasive species are non-native species also referred to as alien species that cause ecological or economic harm in the new region, they live in. This species can be a microbe, plant, insect or an animal and not all alien species are invasive. Some can be domesticated or are kept in zoos. It is when such a group starts living on its own in the new area (becomes feral) and poses a significant threat to local species or starts causing damage to property, health and well being of people is when they are considered invasive. In the case of cats in Antarctica (it is not just Antarctica cats are invasive in) they were brought to Antarctica as pets of researchers and Pablo Escobar’s hippos were part of his private zoo. In both cases there has been significant damage caused to the local ecosystem.
At the root of all biological invasions is human travel and trade. Other factors like availability of perfect climate, easy source of food and a secure space for living facilitate growth in the population of that species.
According to IPBES (Intergovernmental Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services) over $423 billion is the estimated global annual economic cost of dealing with problems associated with invasive species which is certainly a very high number! What leads to this becoming such a costly issue? Well, that depends on the species, the new habitat and several factors that have to come together to create an invasion. So let’s look at the journey of two species which have very interesting stories. The Burmese Python and the Rock Dove (known mainly as the pigeons we city dwellers see every single day).

Burmese Python

Size: 16-23 feet

Features: Powerful constrictor, non-venomous, excellent swimmer and a relatively docile disposition which makes them a popular choice for reptile owners.

Introduction in Everglades National Park, Florida
They were likely pets released in the Everglades but it was after Hurricane Andrew (1992) that the population became significant. The hurricane destroyed a python breeding facility that released them in neighbouring swamps.
The challenge of managing their population in the Everglades lies in the fact that the area is quite difficult to access. Pythons by nature are also good at hiding and resilient creatures. So detecting them is a difficult task.
Impact
In the Everglades, they pose a threat to the native ecosystem. They outcompete native wildlife for food resources and can become resistant to predation as they grow very quickly. They also have a varied diet so several species can serve as a food source. Primarily mammalian (animals like raccoons, fox, rabbits, etc.) populations have seen a significant decline. Even an American Alligator can serve as prey or predator depending on the size of individuals. Another reason for their population growth is that a single female can lay a clutch of 20 - 50 eggs every year!
Control
To control population, several methods have been used. Trap setting, having dog search teams and hunts have been organised to minimal effect. A proposed method of control might be reintroduction of native predators. As of Jan 2012 importing Burmese pythons in the US is illegal.
Native population
The native region of Burmese Pythons is south and southeast Asia as shown in the map below. It is invasive in the US and Puerto Rico. At the same time this python species is declared ‘Vulnerable’ by the IUCN Redlist in its native habitat.

Rock Dove

Size: 30–35 cm

Features: Fast breeder, thrives in cities

Countries where pigeons are declared invasive
Wild, Feral and domesticated
Like other invasive species, it’s important to distinguish between where Rock Doves are native and where they’ve become invasive. But with Rock Doves, there’s an added twist…genetics. These birds are the wild ancestors of the feral pigeons we see thriving in cities today. They’re the same species, but we call the wild/ native populations Rock Doves, while the city-dwelling ones that live independently are Feral Pigeons.
Homing Instinct
Domestication of Rock Doves over centuries has created significant distinction in the genetics of these pigeons. Feral pigeons or pigeons we see in cities are descendants of domesticated pigeons. They look like their wild counterparts and but can have variation in colours.
Invasive in native countries as well
Rock dove are native to Europe, Northern Africa and Western Asia. In its native regions the challenge is keeping the feral population separate from the wild population. Feral pigeons can act as vectors for diseases that can impact humans and livestock. While generally they might not cause major diseases in humans, they can transmit diseases to other native birds. They also outcompete native birds with respect to food sources. Pigeon droppings also cause significant property damage.
The Urban Pigeon
Pigeons have pretty much become a feature of urban life. No city skyline is complete without the fluttering of pigeon wings. Certain city squares like the Washington Square Park in New York City are known for their pigeon populations. While laws are being implemented in countries to deter feeding pigeons not everyone is happy with it. While there are good and bad things associated with bird feeding in general the culture introduces another level of complexity to the problem

Once domesticated then feral and now urban the Rock Doves or pigeons are a feature of today's urban skies and invasive almost worldwide. Pigeons have a long history with humans and have become a part of our urban cultures. So the case of pigeons introduces the ‘cultural’ aspect to the problem of invasive species.

Global Impact

While invasive species do not follow political boundaries the policies regarding the status and management of a species is regulated by national laws. Factors that seem to contribute to an alien species becoming invasive are trade, travel, presence of appropriate climatic condition, an easy source of food and secure space for living. Trade and travel result in the introduction of alien species and the environment facilitates population growth. Some inherent qualities of the species like being difficult to locate or quick adaptations tend to also play a role.

Insights

Most of the top 15 countries with the highest number of invasive species also have the highest GDPs. So the link between number of invasive species in a country with trade is further strengthened. Islands like Réunion and New Caledonia also make the list probably because island ecosystems are more closed off and sensitive to new species. Countries like New Zealand and South Africa have a history of deliberate and accidental introduction of alien species. They are also countries that have regions with subtropical climate. South Africa is also ecologically very diverse, so programs have been developed to protect native ecological systems.

What Kinds of Species Go Invasive?

This scatter plot above shows different types of species (grouped by phylum) on the horizontal axis, and the number of countries they are invasive in on the vertical axis. The dots are colour coded into 4 categories dividing species in clusters (Local Nuisance (invasive in few countries), On the Radar (invasive in more than 15), Explorer (invasive in more than 38) and Invasive Everywhere(invasive in over 85)).

When we look for the species that is invasive in most countries, pigeons win the race by being invasive in 184 countries (there are 195 countries in the world).
If we focus just on plants and animals, the types of species that most often become invasive include flowering plants, vertebrates, and insects. This makes sense as these are groups that humans interact with frequently, and they can easily cause ecological or economic harm.
Interestingly, the cluster labelled “Invasive Everywhere” is also made up mostly of flowering plants, vertebrates, and insects. Within this group, there’s a wide range in how many countries each species has spread to, which could suggest that these species have a long history of human movement, trade, or use which gives them more chances to spread and establish in new places.

End of the Trail - For Now

Invasive species are a global problem but solutions require local intervention and an understanding of factors influencing the system. So first and foremost, awareness about this helps with preventing creation of new invasions and further spread of existing ones. Prevention is also the most low-cost way of managing this issue.
Since each invasion is different depending on the place, solving the problem means understanding not just the local environment, but sometimes the culture too. Learning from how other places have handled similar issues can also be helpful. It’s important to understand the species involved, the people affected, and to work with those who know the issue well (like researchers, volunteers, and policymakers). Dealing with invasive species can be risky, both for the people doing the work and the animals themselves. That’s why it’s so important to have the right people and organizations involved to find the most effective and humane solutions.
While we refer to this problem as ‘invasions’ they are a problem that humans have created. Human activities are linked to all biological invasions. So keeping this in mind when we think of all problems invasive species create is also important.
